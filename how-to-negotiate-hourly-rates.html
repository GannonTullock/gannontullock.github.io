How To Break Down and Negotiate Hourly Rates

Gannon Tullock  |  February 5, 2026

Despite new commercial models emerging, hourly rates are still how the majority of agency work is priced and charged today.

During my time in procurement I found they could be difficult to negotiate if I didn’t have an exact benchmark or apples to apple comparison. They show up in rate cards or SOWs as a single number, with very little explanation behind how that number was created.

From the agency side, though, hourly rates are not arbitrary. They are the result of a fairly standard formula that agencies use.

This article walks through the formula in plain terms, starting with the fundamentals, then explaining how the hourly rate is calculated, and ending with how best to negotiate them.

Step 1: Base salary

The foundation of hourly rates is the base salary.

This is the annual compensation paid to the employee for their role. This usually does not include social costs/benefits and bonuses, but it is important to verify that with the agency as some use a “loaded salary” which includes the above.

Example:
An Account Supervisor earns a base salary of $100,000 per year.

At this stage, nothing has been converted to an hourly rate. We are still working in annual numbers.

Step 2: Overhead

Salary alone is not the full cost of an employee to the agency. Overhead captures all the additional costs required to employ and support that person.

Common components of overhead include:

  • Indirect Payroll
  • Health insurance and benefits
  • Retirement contributions
  • Paid time off
  • Training and professional development
  • Office space or remote work stipends
  • Software, tools, and licenses
  • Finance, HR, legal, and IT support

Overhead is usually expressed as a percentage of base salary.

Typical ranges vary by agency type and geography but often fall somewhere between 80% and 120%.

Example:
Base salary: $100,000
Overhead at 110%: $110,000

Total fully loaded cost so far: $210,000

Step 3: Profit

After salary and overhead are accounted for, agencies add profit.

Profit is not a hidden fee. It is what allows a business to:

  • Invest in growth
  • Absorb risk
  • Fund innovation and new offerings
  • Remain financially viable long term

In many agency models, profit is calculated as a percentage of the total fully loaded cost (salary + overhead + profit).

This means profit is embedded in the total, rather than simply added on top.

Example:
Total cost before profit: $210,000

If profit represents 15% of total cost:
Total cost including profit = $210,000 ÷ (1 − 0.15) = $247,059

Profit amount:
$247,059 − $210,000 = $37,059

Total annual cost including profit: $247,059

Step 4: Hours per FTE

Hours per FTE is the estimated number of hours that employee will work throughout the year.

The standard that agencies often push for is 1,800 hrs/FTE while reconciliation reports show that the real number of hours worked falls between 1,900 and 2,100 hrs/FTE based on agency type and market.

A 100-hour difference may not sound large, but it has a meaningful impact on the resulting hourly rate.

Example assumptions:

  • Brand model: 1,900 hours
  • Agency model: 1,800 hours

Step 5: Calculating the final hourly rate

Putting it all together, it’s time to calculate the hourly rate

  1. Start with base salary
  2. Add overhead
  3. Add profit
  4. Divide by hours per FTE

Using the full example:

Base salary: $100,000
Overhead: $110,000
Profit: $37,059

Total annual cost: $247,059

Using 1,900 hours (Brand):
$247,059 ÷ 1,900 = $130 per hour (rounded)

Using 1,800 hours (Agency):
$247,059 ÷ 1,800 = $137 per hour (rounded)

That is how the final hourly rate charged to a brand is typically calculated.

How To Negotiate Hourly Rates

The benefit of understanding how hourly rates are built is that you can now ask for more transparency from your agency.

Instead of just receiving a rate card, ask to understand their overhead, profit margins, hours per FTE, and employee locations.

With this level of detail, you’re able to compare the components to internal and external benchmarks and have data-backed conversations with your agency.

The goal isn’t to drive every rate to the lowest possible number. The goal is to understand what you’re paying for and why.

Ensuring the correct number of hours from the right people are tied to the SOW is just as important, but I’ll save that for the next article.
